What I Did

Data

  • 10 years of monthly imports (2015-2025)
  • 3 countries: China, Mexico, Canada
  • Focus: Transportation equipment
  • Source: Federal Reserve (FRED), Census Bureau

Method

  • Difference-in-Differences approach
  • Treatment: China (tariffs applied)
  • Control: Mexico & Canada (smaller tariffs)
  • Natural experiment: Section 301 (2018) + Universal (2025)

Why Difference-in-Differences?

Compares how Chinese imports changed relative to control countries, isolating the causal effect of tariffs

  • Used high-quality federal data spanning a decade
  • Natural experiment: tariffs were imposed hard on China, changed for Mexico/Canada
  • This lets us isolate the CAUSAL effect of tariffs
  • Transportation equipment: $40B+/month sector, critical for supply chains
  • Difference-in-differences: Gold standard method for policy evaluation

WHY DIFFERENCE-IN-DIFFERENCES (vs other methods)?

The Research Question Requires Causal Inference: - Question: “Did tariffs CAUSE import reductions?” (not just correlation) - Need to isolate tariff effect from other factors (economy, technology, demand) - DiD provides CAUSAL estimates by comparing relative changes - This is a “what caused what” question, not a “predict what happens” question

DiD vs Machine Learning (Random Forest, Neural Networks, etc.):

Why NOT Random Forest/ML for this question: 1. BLACK BOX PROBLEM: Can’t explain WHY tariffs worked, just predict outcomes - RF gives feature importance, but doesn’t explain causal mechanisms - Neural networks are even worse - completely opaque 2. NO CAUSAL INFERENCE: ML finds correlations, not causes - Random Forest might predict “China imports will decline” - But CAN’T tell us if tariffs CAUSED it vs other factors (recession, tech change, etc.) - Could be spurious correlation 3. OVERFITTING RISK: ML models might capture noise as signal - Example: ML might find “imports decline when oil prices rise” - But that’s just correlation in this dataset, not a general relationship - Especially risky with limited time series data 4. NO COUNTERFACTUAL: ML can’t answer “what would have happened WITHOUT tariffs?” - This is THE KEY question for policy evaluation - DiD’s control group provides this counterfactual directly 5. INTERPRETABILITY: Policymakers need clear answers - “Tariffs reduced imports by $10B/month” is actionable - “Random Forest importance score: 0.73” tells them nothing

Why DiD is Superior for Causal Questions: 1. CAUSAL IDENTIFICATION: Natural experiment design isolates treatment effect - Treatment group (China) got tariffs, Control group (Mexico/Canada) didn’t - Compares RELATIVE changes, removing common trends (economy, demand, etc.) - This difference-in-differences IS the causal effect 2. COUNTERFACTUAL ESTIMATION: Control group shows what WOULD HAVE happened - Mexico/Canada show us what China’s imports would have been without tariffs - This is the fundamental problem of causal inference - we can never observe both - DiD solves this with parallel control group 3. TRANSPARENT ASSUMPTIONS: Can TEST key assumption (parallel trends) - Checked 2015-2017: treatment and control moved together (validated!) - ML assumptions (IID, stationarity, etc.) are often untestable - If parallel trends fails, we KNOW DiD won’t work - can see the problem 4. POLICY INTERPRETABLE: Clear, actionable coefficient - “-$10 billion/month” directly answers policy question - Standard errors and confidence intervals show precision - Policymakers understand this immediately 5. ESTABLISHED CREDIBILITY: Gold standard in economics/policy evaluation - Card & Krueger (minimum wage effects) - Autor, Dorn, Hanson (China shock) - Academic reviewers and policymakers trust the method

When WOULD ML Be Better? - PREDICTION questions: “What will imports be next month?” → Random Forest, LSTM - COMPLEX NON-LINEAR patterns: “Which 100 factors predict trade?” → Neural Networks - HIGH-DIMENSIONAL data: Many variables, unclear relationships → ML ensemble - PATTERN RECOGNITION: “Classify trade disruptions” → Classification algorithms - BUT: Our question is CAUSAL (“what caused what?”), not predictive (“what happens next?”) - For causal questions, DiD/econometrics >> ML every time

Other Econometric Methods Considered:

Time Series Models (ARIMA, VAR): - Why not: Can’t isolate causal effect of specific policy - Just describes patterns in data over time - Can’t separate tariff impact from recession, tech change, demand shifts - No control group = no counterfactual

Simple Before-After Comparison: - Why not: What if imports were declining anyway? (they weren’t, but we need to prove it) - Can’t attribute changes SPECIFICALLY to tariffs vs other factors - No control group to validate the assumption - This is “correlation” not “causation”

Regression with Many Controls: - Why not: Which controls? How many? (ad hoc choices) - Endogeneity issues (controls might be affected by tariffs too!) - Still no clear counterfactual comparison - DiD IS regression, but with better identification strategy (interaction term!)

Synthetic Control Method: - Why not: Could actually work! Alternative valid approach - Requires longer pre-treatment period (we have enough) - More complex to implement and explain - DiD is simpler, equally valid, more interpretable - For dissertation/advanced work, could try both as robustness

Instrumental Variables: - Why not: What’s the instrument? (need something that affects tariffs but not imports directly) - Hard to find valid instruments - DiD is “reduced form” - directly estimates what we care about - Don’t need the complexity of IV here

Bottom Line on Method Choice: - Research question is fundamentally CAUSAL: “Did tariffs CAUSE import reductions?” - DiD directly answers this with transparent, testable assumptions - ML methods answer different questions (prediction, correlation, classification) - Using Random Forest here would be methodologically inappropriate - “Right tool for the right job” - DiD is THE right tool for causal policy evaluation - This is why it’s used in top economics journals for policy analysis - Card won Nobel Prize in 2021 partly for developing/popularizing DiD!

If Asked “Why Not ML?”: Quick answer: “ML is great for prediction, but I need causal inference. DiD gives me a clear counterfactual - what would have happened to China WITHOUT tariffs - which ML can’t provide. The question isn’t ‘what will happen?’ but ‘what DID the policy cause?’ Different tools for different questions.”

Timeline: Two Major Tariff Events

  • 2015-2017: Baseline period, normal trade patterns
  • July 2018: Section 301 tariffs - 25% on $250B of Chinese goods
  • April 2025: Universal tariffs - 10% baseline + reciprocal rates (China ~69% total)
  • This gives us two natural experiments to study!

Finding #1: Tariffs Work (Bilaterally)

Chinese imports declined 25%

The Numbers

  • -$10 billion per month
  • -25% after Section 301
  • -32% after Universal tariffs
  • Highly significant (p < 0.001)

Robust Evidence

  • Consistent across 5 models
  • R² = 0.85-0.89
  • Validated with Census data
  • Parallel trends confirmed
  • This is the headline: tariffs DO reduce bilateral imports
  • $10 billion per month decline - that’s $120B per year!
  • Effect is immediate and persistent
  • Not a temporary shock - sustained decline over 7 years
  • Statistical evidence is rock solid - can’t be explained by chance
  • Multiple robustness checks all confirm the finding

Visual Evidence: The Divergence

  • Before 2018: both groups move in parallel (validates our approach)
  • After Section 301: China drops sharply, controls stay flat
  • After Universal 2025: China drops even more
  • This visual pattern IS the difference-in-differences effect
  • The gap between the lines is our causal estimate

Finding #2: Trade Diversion

Supply chains relocated

Not a sign of domestic production increase

Where Did Imports Go?

  • Mexico: +30% (from $6.3B to $8.5B/month)
  • Canada: +10% (from $5.5B to $6.0B/month)
  • Net reduction: ~78% (22% diverted)

What This Means

  • Tariffs worked? -️ But not in aggregate
  • USMCA facilitated reallocation
  • Supply chains are flexible!
  • increase on domestic production? no way!!!
  • Key finding: imports didn’t disappear, they moved!
  • Mexico was the big winner - captured most of the diverted trade
  • USMCA (free trade agreement) made Mexico/Canada attractive alternatives
  • This is economically important: if goal was to reduce TOTAL imports, largely failed
  • If goal was to hurt China specifically, it worked
  • But US consumers still buying imported goods - just from different countries

The Heatmap Tells the Story

  • Red = declining imports, Blue = increasing imports
  • China: progressive red (getting worse over time)
  • Mexico: blue throughout (beneficiary!)
  • Canada: lighter blue (modest gains)
  • The story: China loses, neighbors win
  • This is classic trade diversion - textbook case!

Finding #3: Industry Context

Trucking tonnage increased despite import declines

The Puzzle

  • Chinese imports: -$10B/month
  • US trucking tonnage: +9% growth

. . .

The Explanation

  1. Trade diversion - More Mexico/Canada shipments
  2. E-commerce boom - Last-mile delivery surge
  3. Domestic production? - No Way!!!
  • This finding surprised me initially
  • If imports declined so much, why did trucking grow?
  • Answer: Trade diversion! Mexico/Canada shipments grew
  • Plus massive e-commerce growth during COVID
  • Suggests import declines didn’t hurt domestic freight industry
  • Important for policy evaluation - not all doom and gloom

Opportunities

Route Length Comparison: China vs Mexico

  • This is HUGE for understanding owner-operator economics
  • China imports: Arrive at LA/Long Beach, short truck to nearby warehouses (50-200 miles)
  • Same in NJ ports:Arrives at ports, short truck to nearby warehouses, volume is key for it
  • Mexico imports: Cross at Laredo, truck 500-1,750 miles to reach same destinations
  • Example: Chicago distribution center

  • This explains why border-region truckers benefited
  • More miles = more revenue, especially for owner-operators paid by the mile

Border Crossing Trends: Mexico to US

  • This shows the MECHANISM of trade diversion for trucking

Parallel Trends Validation

The Assumption

DiD requires that treatment and control groups would have followed parallel trends absent treatment

The Test

  • Examined 2015-2017 baseline
  • Regression: imports ~ treatment × time
  • Result: No significant difference (p=0.42)
  • This is THE key assumption for causal inference
  • We can test it using pre-treatment data
  • Visual: lines move together before 2018
  • Statistical test confirms: no significant difference
  • This validates our entire approach!

Regression Results

Interpretation

Remarkably consistent estimates (-$9.7B to -$10.1B) across specifications. High R² values (0.85-0.89) indicate strong model fit. Log specification confirms ~25% decline.

  • Five different model specifications
  • All tell the same story: ~$10B decline
  • High R² means models explain 85-89% of variation
  • Log model confirms percentage interpretation
  • Statistical significance far exceeds conventional thresholds
  • This is as robust as econometric evidence gets!
